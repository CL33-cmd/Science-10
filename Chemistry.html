Classifying Matter

  • Matter: anything with mass and volume; may be liquid, solid, or gas
    • Mixture: combination of matter that can be separated by physical means
      • Heterogenous mixture: different components are visible; composition is variable throughout the mixture
      • Homogenous mixture: appears to be a pure substance, composition is constant throughout the mixture
    • Pure Substance: matter that has definite proportion and composition
      • Element: substance that cannot be broken down into smaller substances
      • Compound: two or more elements that are chemically combined; can be separated into smaller parts

Distinguish an atom and an ion of the same atom

  • Ions are charged particles
    • Therefore an ion will have either a positive or a negative charge
    • An atom is balanced, and will not have a charge

Classify an element as a metal, transition metal, or metalloid

  • Metals:solids at room temperature (except mercury), shiny lustre, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable and ductile
    • In the first and second group (except hydrogen) of the periodic table
  • Non-metals: gases and solids at room temperature (except bromine), not very shiny, poor conductors of heat and electricity, brittle, not ductile
    • A "staircase" from Carbon(6), down to Iodine(53), is the start of the non-metals
    • The last group of non-metals is group 17, which starts with Fluorine(9)
  • metalloids: solids at room temperature, some shiny, some dull, somewhat conduct electricity, poor conductors of heat, brittle, not ductile
    • On the other side of the "staircase" are the metalloids. From Boron(5), to astatine(85)
    • The metalloids are: Boron(B), Silicon(Si), Germanium(Ge), Arsenic(As), Antimony(Sb), and Astatine(At)
  • Transition metals are located in the center of the periodic table

List the properties of acids and bases

  • Acids: substances dissolved in water to produce hydrogen ions
    • taste sour
    • pH<7
    • turns blue litmus paper red
    • neutralize bases
    • good conductors of electricity
  • Bases: substances dissolved in water to produce hydroxide ions
    • tastes bitter
    • feel slippery
    • pH>7
    • turn red litmus paper blue
    • neutralize acids
    • good conductors of electricity

Distinguish between ionic compounds, molecular compounds, acids, and bases

  • Ionic Bonds: formed between a metal and a non-metal
    • Process:
      1. electrons are transferred from metal to non-metal
      2. cation and anion form
      3. strong ionic bond forms due to force of attraction between opposing charges
    • Characteristics:
      • forms a crystalline lattice of alternating positive and negative charges
      • solids at room temperature
      • high melting points
      • good conductors of electricity when melted or dissolved in water
      • form very stable compounds
  • Molecular/covalent Bonds: formed between nonmetals
    • share electrons
    • form molecular compounds
    • Characteristics:
      • not a crystalline lattice structure
      • solids, liquids, or gases at room temperature
      • low melting point
      • most do not dissolve in water
      • poor conductors due to absence of ions
      • stability varies
    • Diatomic molecules: covalent bonds between two non-metals of same element
    • Polyatomic molecules: covalent bonds between three or more non-metals of the same element
  • Acids and Bases:
    • dissolve in water and release either hydrogen, or hydroxide ions
    • bases neutralize acids and vice versa; they neutralize each other simultaneously
    • always solutions; cannot be an acid or a base if they are a solid or gas
    • pH of 7 is neither a base nor an acid; it is a neutral substance such as water
    • Naming:
      • state is always aqueous solution (aq)
      • aqueous = dissolved in water
      • cannot be an acid or base it it is not dissolved in water
      • always has H (hydro) as first substance

Distinguish between an endo- and exothermic reaction

  • Chemical bonding:
    • energy is required to break a bond
    • energy is released when a bond forms
  • Endothermic reactions:
    • energy is required from the surrounding to keep a reaction going
    • energy is required to break bonds is greater than the energy released when new bonds from
    • surroundings are cooler after reaction
  • Exothermic reactions:
    • all chemical equations have reactants and products which are expressed reactants > products
    • surroundings are warmer after reaction

Classify a chemical reaction

  • Decomposition:
    • a single reactant breaks down into smaller components
    • Ex. 2H2O(l) --electricity-> 2H2(g) + O2(g)
  • Single Replacement:
    • an element and an ionic compound react to form a different compound and element
    • A + BC > AC + B
    • metal reactant > cations switch
      • 2N(s) + 2HCl(aq) > 2NCl2(aq) + H2
    • non-metal reactant > anions switch
      • 2NaBr(aq) + Cl2(g) > 2NaCl(aq) + Br2(l)
  • Double Replacement:
    • two ionic compounds react to form two new different ionic compounds
    • the cations and anions switch partners
      • AB + CD > CB + AD
  • Combustion
    • a hydrocarbon reacts with oxygen to produce CO2 and water vapor
    • Complete Combustion: hydrocarbon burns efficiently, very little residue left behind
    • Incomplete Combustion: insufficient oxygen is available to react, lots of smoke and soot produced, carbon monoxide produced instead of carbon dioxide

Calculate molar mass of a substance

  • Moles: amount of substance that contains the same number of formula units as there are atoms in a 12g sample of Carbon-12
    • in a 12g sample of Carbon-12 there are 6.02 x 10^23 carbon atoms
    • 1 mole of substance therefore contains 6.02 x 10^23 atoms, ions, molecules, or particles
    • the number 6.02 x 10^23 is called Avogadro's Number
    • units for moles is abbreviated as "mol"
      • 1 mol = 6.02 x 10^23 formula units
  • Calculating moles: can be determined through a unit analysis using: n = P/NA
    • Where:
      • n = number of moles of substance
      • P = number of atoms, ions, molecules, or particles that make up the substance
      • NA = Avogadro's number

Calculate between moles, mass, and molar mass

  • Atomic molar mass: the mass of a given pure substance (g) divided by amount of substance (mol)
    • the mass of one mole of a pure substance
    • Ex. water has a molar mass of 18.02 g/mol
      • this means that 1 mol, or 6.02 x 10^23 molecules of water have a total mass of 16 g/mol
  • Calculating mass:
    • unit analysis using: n=m/M
      • Where:
        • n = number of moles of the substance (mol)
        • m = mass of substance (g)
        • M = molar mass of substance (g/mol)
